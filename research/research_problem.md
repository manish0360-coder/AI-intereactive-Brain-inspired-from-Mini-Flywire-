## 1. Problem Statement

Artificial intelligence has achieved remarkable success through increasingly large models, datasets, and computational resources. Modern systems can generate text, images, software, and scientific hypotheses with unprecedented capability. However, despite these advances, a fundamental scientific question remains unresolved.

Current AI research primarily evaluates systems through external behavior—benchmark scores, task performance, and output quality. While these metrics measure capability, they provide limited understanding of the internal computational mechanisms that enable systems to acquire, reorganize, and apply knowledge in novel situations.

MiniFlyWire addresses this gap by treating artificial cognition as a scientific object of study rather than solely an engineering objective. The central research problem is to identify, isolate, and experimentally validate the computational mechanisms through which experience is transformed into reusable, structured, and constraint-aware knowledge capable of supporting the construction of novel solutions.

Rather than proposing a fixed cognitive architecture or claiming a predefined path toward artificial general intelligence, MiniFlyWire seeks to establish a rigorous experimental methodology for investigating how internal representations evolve, how knowledge structures emerge, and which mechanisms causally contribute to robust generalization, adaptation, and invention.

The objective is not merely to build increasingly capable artificial systems, but to develop experimentally validated scientific principles that explain how artificial cognitive systems progressively acquire, organize, reconstruct, and apply knowledge across diverse environments and engineering domains.

## 2. Why Existing AI Systems Fail

Current artificial intelligence has achieved remarkable capability through advances in deep learning, large-scale data, reinforcement learning, and generative modeling. These approaches have demonstrated impressive performance across language, vision, robotics, scientific reasoning, and engineering design.

However, despite these successes, existing research methodologies remain primarily optimized for maximizing external task performance rather than explaining the computational mechanisms responsible for that performance. Benchmark accuracy, reward, loss functions, and generation quality measure what a system can accomplish, but provide limited evidence regarding how internal knowledge structures emerge, reorganize, interact, and support increasingly novel problem solving.

As a consequence, many cognitive mechanisms—including memory organization, abstraction, causal reasoning, representation restructuring, and knowledge transfer—are often evaluated indirectly through behavioral improvements rather than through controlled causal experiments. Multiple architectures may achieve similar performance while relying upon fundamentally different internal computational processes, making it difficult to determine which mechanisms genuinely contribute to robust cognition.

This challenge becomes particularly significant in engineering and manufacturing domains. Generating plausible solutions is insufficient when designs must simultaneously satisfy physical laws, logical consistency, manufacturability, safety requirements, resource constraints, and long-term reliability. Existing evaluation methodologies provide limited insight into how internal computational mechanisms construct, verify, and adapt knowledge while preserving such constraints.

MiniFlyWire does not seek to replace existing artificial intelligence methodologies or propose another cognitive architecture. Instead, it introduces a complementary scientific methodology centered on causal mechanism evaluation. Rather than treating entire architectures as the primary unit of analysis, MiniFlyWire treats individual computational mechanisms as experimentally testable scientific hypotheses whose contributions can be isolated, measured, compared, and validated across different cognitive architectures.

The central objective of the laboratory is therefore not to determine which architecture performs best, but to determine which computational mechanisms are causally responsible for engineering-capable cognition. By shifting the focus from architecture-centric evaluation to mechanism-centric experimentation, MiniFlyWire aims to establish a reproducible scientific methodology for cumulative cognitive architecture research.

## 3. Central Hypothesis

The central hypothesis of the MiniFlyWire research program is that engineering-capable artificial cognition does not emerge from a single algorithm or isolated cognitive capability. Instead, it emerges from the coordinated interaction of multiple computational mechanisms that construct, evaluate, refine, and reuse internal computational models while satisfying explicit environmental, logical, physical, and task-specific constraints.

We further hypothesize that these mechanisms are individually measurable, experimentally isolatable through controlled ablation and intervention studies, and collectively sufficient to explain progressively more capable engineering reasoning. By identifying the causal contribution of each mechanism, MiniFlyWire aims to transform cognitive architecture design from heuristic engineering into an experimentally grounded scientific discipline.

Rather than assuming which mechanisms are necessary, the laboratory will treat every proposed cognitive component as a falsifiable scientific hypothesis. Mechanisms that fail to demonstrate measurable causal benefit under controlled experimental conditions will be rejected regardless of their intuitive appeal or biological inspiration.

---

## 4. Falsification Criteria

The central hypothesis of MiniFlyWire will be considered scientifically unsupported if one or more of the following conditions are consistently demonstrated through controlled experimentation.

1. Equivalent engineering reasoning can be achieved without the proposed cognitive mechanisms, indicating that the mechanisms are not causally necessary for the observed capability.

2. Independent experiments fail to identify measurable causal contributions from individual computational mechanisms despite controlled isolation, intervention, and ablation studies.

3. The proposed mechanisms fail to provide measurable scientific value beyond existing approaches, including improvements in mechanistic interpretability, causal explanatory power, constraint preservation, reproducibility, or experimentally verifiable understanding of engineering-capable cognition.

Behavioral performance alone shall not be considered sufficient evidence for or against a proposed cognitive mechanism. Scientific value must instead be evaluated through the laboratory's ability to explain, isolate, validate, and reproduce the causal contribution of individual computational mechanisms.


4. The laboratory fails to produce reproducible evidence explaining how internal computational mechanisms contribute to engineering-capable cognition, despite extensive instrumentation and analysis.

5. Simpler computational models consistently explain experimental observations with equal or greater predictive power, violating the principle of scientific parsimony.

If these conditions are satisfied, the current theoretical framework must be revised or rejected. MiniFlyWire therefore treats every cognitive mechanism as a provisional scientific hypothesis rather than an established fact.

---


## 5. Expected Scientific Contributions

If the central hypothesis is experimentally supported, MiniFlyWire will contribute a reproducible scientific methodology for investigating artificial cognition beyond behavioral evaluation alone. Rather than treating cognitive architectures as monolithic engineering systems, the laboratory will establish a framework for isolating, measuring, and experimentally validating the causal contribution of individual computational mechanisms.

The expected outcome is not a single cognitive architecture, but a growing body of experimentally supported evidence describing which computational mechanisms consistently contribute to robust learning, knowledge organization, reasoning, planning, adaptation, and engineering problem solving under explicit constraints.

This methodology is intended to provide a scientific foundation for future artificial cognitive systems. The validated mechanisms developed and evaluated within MiniFlyWire will directly inform the design of Noetica, support the engineering intelligence developed in Velith, and ultimately contribute toward the long-term realization of Mini Prometheus as an autonomous manufacturing intelligence.

More broadly, MiniFlyWire seeks to establish cognitive architecture research as a cumulative experimental science in which hypotheses are continuously tested, revised, rejected, or strengthened through reproducible evidence rather than intuition or benchmark performance alone.
